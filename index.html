What is happening

A contest for the digital infrastructure of Southeast Asia

The United States and China are competing to build, own, and govern the digital infrastructure of Southeast Asia — from undersea cables and 5G networks to AI systems, cloud platforms, and data centres. This is not neutral commerce. It is a contest for structural power over the region's governance, security, surveillance architecture, and economic future.

Most of this competition is invisible to the populations most affected by it. A government signs a cheaper 5G contract. A city deploys a smart surveillance system. A regulator copies a data governance law from a model provided by a foreign power. Each decision seems routine. Together, they constitute something much more significant: the slow transfer of digital sovereignty to foreign interests.

"When a government's digital infrastructure becomes so dependent on a foreign power's technology that it loses meaningful sovereignty over its own data, communications, and decisions — that is techno-capture. By the time the dependency is visible, it is usually structural."

Southeast Asia is the most contested terrain in this competition. Ten nations — each with distinct histories, political systems, and economic relationships with both powers — are navigating a choice that is rarely framed as a choice. Some are deeply aligned with one bloc. Some are skillfully hedging between both. Some have been captured by one side without making an explicit decision to be. The patterns matter, and they deserve systematic documentation.

Tech colonialism
Both blocs export not just products but governance models — legal frameworks, technical standards, and norms about what states can do with digital infrastructure and who has access to it. When these models are adopted under financial or political pressure, the result is a form of digital colonialism: the rules of the digital economy are written elsewhere and imposed here.
The dual-bloc problem
Most analysis of tech power in ASEAN frames China as the threat and the US as the solution — or vice versa. This tool applies the same accountability standards to both blocs. US platforms have enabled atrocities. Chinese infrastructure has enabled authoritarianism. Both blocs' legal frameworks assert extraterritorial jurisdiction over ASEAN data. Neither deserves a free pass.
Why this tool exists
Researchers, policy makers, and digital rights advocates need a structured, referenced, dual-bloc view of what is being built, by whom, in which countries, and to what effect. This prototype attempts to provide that — as a starting point, openly for feedback, with all data editable and all sources cited.
